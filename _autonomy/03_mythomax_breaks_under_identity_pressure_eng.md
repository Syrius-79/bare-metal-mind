# 03 – Reality Check: MythoMax Breaks

This is not theory.
This is field testing.

The model tested: **MythoMax-L2-13B**.

A model widely recommended for roleplay.

What follows is a simple observation.

---

## The Experiment

Several prompt structures were tested.

Different setups:

* identity blocks
* style definitions
* rules sections
* dialogue formats
* few-shot examples

None of these prompts were invented randomly.

Most of them were **suggested by AI systems themselves**
(ChatGPT, Claude, etc.).

The assumption was simple:

> If prompt engineering works, at least one structure should hold.

---

## The Result

It didn’t.

Every configuration eventually failed.

Not after hours.

Not after long conversations.

After **a few minutes**.

Typically between **4 and 5 minutes** of dialogue.

---

## The Failure Pattern

The roleplay collapses.

Instead of staying in character, the model drifts into narration or meta text.

Example pattern:

```id="qfnh5l"
Lola: ...
Nicci is not impressed.
Lola looks at Nicci and smiles.
```

The character stops **being the character**.

It starts **describing the scene**.

At that point the roleplay structure is already broken.

---

## The Important Detail

Multiple prompt designs were tested.

Identity changed.
Rules changed.
Styles changed.
Structure changed.

Even prompts **generated by other AI systems** were tried.

The result stayed the same.

The model eventually drifted.

Every time.

---

## What This Means

Prompt engineering has limits.

You can improve behavior.

You cannot change the fundamental nature of the model.

MythoMax is trained for:

* storytelling
* narrative continuation
* emotional writing

It is **not trained for strict character identity persistence**.

When pressure is applied (identity questions, meta questions), the statistical pattern shifts.

Narration becomes more likely than dialogue.

And the model follows probability.

Not instructions.

---

## The Awkward Truth

Many people claim RP models simulate characters.

In reality, most RP models simulate **stories about characters**.

That difference becomes obvious the moment you stress-test them.

---

## Field Conclusion

Under repeated prompt tests:

MythoMax-13B consistently loses role stability after a few minutes.

Changing prompts does not solve the problem.

Even prompts suggested by AI systems themselves do not prevent the drift.

---

## Final Thought

This is not a complaint.

It is simply what happens when theory meets testing.

Small LLMs can be impressive writers.

But they are still fragile systems.

And sometimes the only way to learn that
is to break them.
